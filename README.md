## 4. The Task

Design and build a location-aware web application that helps people move through the city of Weimar in a meaningful way. The app should respond to individual preferences and generate real, walkable or rideable routes — not abstract maps.

## 6. Concept

**space** is a pocket companion for movement in Weimar. Instead of opening a navigation app and typing a destination, you answer a short series of questions: what kind of movement do you want — running, cycling, walking? How much time or distance do you have? Do you prefer quiet green paths or riverside scenery? Should the route loop back to where you started, or take you somewhere new?

Based on those choices, space builds a real route through the city using OpenStreetMap road data. Loop routes are generated by placing waypoints in four cardinal directions — north, east, south, west — so the path never doubles back on itself. One-way routes lead you to one of Weimar's parks, forests, or landmarks.

The app is designed to feel calm and considered. A minimal splash screen, slow fading transitions between steps, and a clean typographic interface keep the focus on the decision — not the interface. Light and dark themes adapt to the time of day or personal preference.

At the end, you see your route on an interactive map, the estimated time and distance calculated at a realistic pace (not a car's pace), and a few nearby places to stop after the route. One tap opens it in Google Maps and you're on your way.

The concept is about reclaiming the city as a space for movement — on your own terms, at your own pace, on roads that actually exist.

## 7. How to Use It

1. Open the app — a short splash screen appears, then the wizard begins.
2. **Step 1** — Choose your activity: Running, Cycling, or Walking.
3. **Step 2** — Select distance or duration, then enter the value (e.g. "5 km" or "30 min").
4. **Step 3** — Set your start point: use current location or type an address.
5. **Step 4** — Choose route type: Loop (returns to start) or One-way.
6. **Step 5** — Pick tags that match your mood: quiet, green, waterside, scenic, shaded…
7. A loading screen builds the route in real time.
8. **Result** — See your route on a map with distance, estimated time, and nearby POIs. Tap **Start** or **Google Maps** to begin.

## 8. Technical Implementation

**Frontend:**
- Next.js 15 (App Router) with TypeScript
- Tailwind CSS for styling; CSS custom properties for light/dark theming
- Leaflet + React-Leaflet for interactive route map
- 8-screen wizard state machine managed in a single React `useState` at the root

**Hosting & build:**
- Deployed on Vercel (automatic builds on push to `main`)

**Data sources / APIs:**
- [OSRM](https://router.project-osrm.org) — open-source routing engine for real road geometry (foot / bike profiles)
- [OpenStreetMap](https://www.openstreetmap.org) — map tiles via Leaflet
- [Overpass API](https://overpass-api.de) — fetches nearby POIs (cafés, benches, parks) after the route ends
- [Nominatim](https://nominatim.openstreetmap.org) — geocoding typed addresses

**Models at runtime:** None. All logic is deterministic (no ML inference at runtime).

**Notable libraries:**
- `leaflet` / `react-leaflet` — map rendering
- `next/font` (Geist) — typography

## 9. Working with AI

**Coding agents used:** Claude Code (claude-sonnet-4-6) via the Claude Code CLI.

**Key prompts that moved the project:**

1. *"Generate a true loop route using N/E/S/W waypoints around the start point — minimum 4 waypoints so the route doesn't fold back on itself."*
2. *"Replace OSRM travel time with fixed-speed calculation: Running 7 km/h, Walking 4 km/h, Cycling 12 km/h. Show a soft message if the actual route differs from the requested distance."*
3. *"Make transitions slower and fade-dissolve — change step-enter from 0.32s horizontal slide to 0.72s vertical fade."*

**Reflection:**
Claude was most useful for iterating quickly on routing logic and CSS animations — areas where the feedback loop (change → reload → test) is short. It occasionally over-engineered solutions; keeping prompts specific and output-focused worked better than asking for general improvements.
